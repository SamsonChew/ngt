Algorithm as Sovereignty

You think this algorithm is global. It isn't.

A five-minute scroll on why the recommendation feed in your pocket is less like a neutral piece of technology and more like a border, a flag, a claim on your attention made by whichever state happens to own it.

scroll

Act I · What it actually does

An algorithm doesn't just show you reality. It produces it.

In 2024, TikTok's recommendation system picked up early engagement signals around a small vinyl toy called Labubu and pushed it into the feeds of millions of users who had never searched for it. Within weeks it was sold out worldwide.

The algorithm did not reflect demand. It created demand. A system of computational rules, headquartered in Beijing, decided what tens of millions of people in America and Europe found desirable that year.

That is not a neutral technical process. That is cultural power.

Act II · The asymmetry

Same mechanism. Different owner. Different outcome.

Meta and TikTok run structurally identical recommendation systems. Samsung and Huawei make structurally identical phones. Flip the switch and watch what a government calls a threat.

Act III · January 2026

The deal that revealed the argument.

When the US finally closed the TikTok deal, look at what it insisted on — and what it didn't.

  1. Data

    American user data moved to Oracle servers, on US soil.

    secured

  2. Ownership

    A consortium of American investors took majority ownership of the US entity.

    secured

  3. The algorithm

    Never transferred. Licensed from ByteDance, which kept the intellectual property. The mechanism that decides what 170 million Americans see every day is still, in its design and logic, a ByteDance product.

    licensed · not sold

The US didn't acquire the algorithm. It acquired oversight of it.

If algorithms were truly neutral and technical, it wouldn't matter who owned them. The fact that the deal was fought over the algorithm — not the data, not the hardware — is the clearest admission that the algorithm is a political instrument.

In one sentence

The algorithm was never cosmopolitan.
It was always sovereign.
We just didn't see it — because the algorithms we were used to happened to belong to us.

Three ideas this piece leans on:

  • The belief, after the philosopher Samuel Scheffler, that what really matters crosses borders — that our most important community is humanity as a whole. Technology is often assumed to embody this ideal.
  • Edward Said argued that no depiction of a people or a place is innocent — it is always produced from a position of power. An algorithm, similarly, doesn't show you a neutral world; it shows you one curated from somewhere.
  • Michel-Rolph Trouillot's term for when a particular group's way of doing things becomes so dominant that it stops looking particular and starts looking like the default. American platforms look neutral because we've stopped noticing their nationality.
