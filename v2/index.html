Algorithm as Sovereignty

You think this algorithm is global. It isn't.

A five-minute scroll on why the recommendation feed in your pocket — the representational layer of your digital life — is less like a neutral piece of technology and more like a border, a flag, a claim on your attention made by whichever state happens to own it.

Representational layer — the part of the tech stack that decides what you see, as distinct from the material layer (chips, servers, cables). Phones and silicon sit in one layer; feeds and algorithms sit in another. States can tolerate foreign control of one without the other — until they can’t.
scroll

First, a small story about a toy.

Act I · What it actually does

An algorithm doesn't just show you reality. It produces it.

In 2024, TikTok's recommendation system picked up early engagement signals around a small vinyl toy called Labubu and pushed it into the feeds of millions of users who had never searched for it. Within weeks it was sold out worldwide.1

The algorithm did not reflect demand. It created demand. A system of computational rules, headquartered in Beijing, decided what tens of millions of people in America and Europe found desirable that year.

That is not a neutral technical process. That is cultural power .

The ability to shape what a population finds normal, desirable, or worth arguing about — not by forcing them, but by setting the frame in which their attention moves.

Edward Said argued, decades ago, that no representation of a people or a place is innocent — it is always produced from a position of power, and the more dominant that power, the more its representation starts to feel like simply objective truth.8 A recommendation algorithm does the same work, at industrial scale. It produces a version of reality that feels personalised and neutral — but it is shaped by the institutional, commercial, and ultimately political context of whoever designed it.

If the algorithm produces reality, the question becomes — whose reality, produced by whom, permitted by whom?

Act II · The asymmetry

Same mechanism. Different owner. Different outcome.

Meta and TikTok run structurally identical recommendation systems. Samsung and Huawei make structurally identical phones. Flip the switch and watch what a government chooses to call a threat.

The technology is structurally identical. The ownership is not. What changes between the two sides of this toggle is not the machine — it is whose flag flies above it.

Michel-Rolph Trouillot had a name for this kind of invisibility: the North Atlantic universal .9 When a particular group’s way of doing things becomes so dominant, it stops looking particular. Meta and Google are not seen as American companies making decisions about what billions of people see. They are seen as neutral infrastructure — just how the internet works. TikTok broke that illusion, not because it did something different, but because it made the assumption visible.

A particular group’s practices so dominant they appear universal — the ground, not a position on it. It takes an outsider achieving the same scale to make the ground visible again.

So why this platform? Why not Shein, or WeChat, or Honor phones? Three conditions had to converge:

  1. 01

    Operates at the representational layer

    Not chips or cables — the part of the stack that curates what a population sees, believes, desires.

  2. 02

    Mass penetration inside American borders

    Not a diaspora tool, not a niche app. 170 million US users — roughly half the country.

  3. 03

    Sits at the centre of the US–China tech contest

    Owned by a firm headquartered in the one state the US is actively competing with for control of the global technological stack.

All three. Simultaneously. That is the narrow door through which a platform walks to become a sovereignty problem — and why TikTok is not an outlier but the first case where the door was actually opened.

The cleanest way to see what this really is — is to look at where it has been done before, from the opposite direction.

Act III · The inversion

When McDonald's arrived in Singapore, the state welcomed the burger.
What it policed was the meaning.

The sociologist Chua Beng-Huat, writing about consumerism in Singapore, observed that the postcolonial state did not treat cultural imports as threats to be blocked. It treated them as material to be permitted and meaning to be curated.4

The economic import was permitted.
The ideological import was policed.
The product crossed the border. The sovereignty over its meaning did not.

Imported · permitted

  • The burger
  • The shopping mall
  • The coffee chain
  • The TV format

Meaning · policed

  • What counts as "national"
  • Which holidays close the shops
  • Whose values travel with the product
  • Who gets to narrate the import

The TikTok case runs this logic in reverse. For the first time, the United States — the world's dominant cultural exporter — finds itself on the receiving end. And it is responding, almost line-for-line, the way a small postcolonial state would.

The product is allowed to cross the border. The sovereignty over what it means is not. That is not American exceptionalism. That is the universal logic of sovereignty meeting an unfamiliar side of the trade.

And the clearest proof of that logic is in the fine print of the deal itself.

Act IV · January 2026

The deal that revealed the argument.

Most of the public debate was about data. But the data concern, while real, was always a proxy for a deeper anxiety. The real issue was never who holds the data — it was who controls the mechanism that produces what people see, think, and desire. When the deal was finally signed, look at what the US insisted on — and what it didn’t.5

  1. Data

    American user data moved to Oracle servers, on US soil.

    secured

  2. Ownership

    A consortium of American investors took majority ownership of the US entity.

    secured

  3. The algorithm

    Never transferred. Licensed from ByteDance, which kept the intellectual property. The mechanism that decides what 170 million Americans see every day is still, in its design and logic, a ByteDance product.

    licensed · not sold

The US didn't acquire the algorithm.
It acquired oversight of it.

If algorithms were truly neutral and technical, it wouldn’t matter who owned them. The fact that the deal was fought over the algorithm — not the data, not the hardware — is the clearest admission that the algorithm is a political instrument.

The language was security.
The substance was sovereignty.

And the clinching proof is a counterfactual. Had TikTok remained small — had it not reached 170 million American users — the United States would not have bothered. A Chinese-owned algorithm watched by three million people is a curiosity. A Chinese-owned algorithm watched by half the country is a sovereignty problem. The anxiety arose precisely because TikTok succeeded.

Now the hard part — the objection.

Act V · The strongest objection

But isn't this just about security?

Every serious argument should survive its opposition. Here are the three strongest counter-arguments to everything you have just read — and where each one breaks.

01 Algorithms are technical — they optimise for engagement, not ideology. +

Rebuttal: Engagement is not content-neutral. What an algorithm chooses to amplify reflects the training data, the moderation policy, and the cultural assumptions of whoever built it — all of which are shaped by the political and legal environment around the company. "Optimising for engagement" in Beijing, Menlo Park, and Brussels do not produce the same feed.

02 The US acted on genuine national-security grounds — data access, foreign adversary. +

Rebuttal: If data were the real issue, the deal would have ended at Oracle. It didn't. The fight kept going until the algorithm itself was brought under US oversight — and the algorithm contains no user data. The structure of the settlement reveals what the settlement was actually about.

03 Every country regulates foreign platforms — the EU did it first with the DMA and DSA. +

Rebuttal: EU regulation targets behavior — transparency, interoperability, risk mitigation. US action on TikTok targeted ownership. A Chinese-owned platform that behaved identically to Meta was still treated as existentially different. That distinction — behavior vs. ownership — is the argument.

None of these rebuttals require the US to be acting in bad faith. They only require the action to be structurally sovereign — a state reaching for control over what its population can see — even when it is sincerely spoken as security.

Which leaves one sentence.

In one sentence

The algorithm was never cosmopolitan .
It was always sovereign.
We just didn't see it — because the algorithms we were used to happened to belong to us, and had become invisible as political .

Cosmopolitan — after Samuel Scheffler: the belief that what really matters crosses borders, that our most important community is humanity as a whole. Technology is often assumed to embody this ideal.
North Atlantic universal — Michel-Rolph Trouillot's term for when a particular group's way of doing things becomes so dominant that it stops looking particular and starts looking like the default. American platforms look neutral because we've stopped noticing their nationality.

Next time you open your feed —

whose country's logic
is deciding what you see?
